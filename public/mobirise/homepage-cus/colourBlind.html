What is colour blind?

Colour blind (color vision deficiency) is a fairly common condition in which people do not see colours in the traditional way. This happens when cones (a type of nerve cell in your eye retina) are not working correctly. Cones process light and images as they enter the eye and send signals to the brain that allow to perceive the colour.

Colour blind usually does not mean that people can not see any colours. The vast majority of people with colour blind see a range of colours, but they see some colours differently than others do. They may also have trouble telling the difference between certain colours or shades. Some very rare forms of colour blind make a person unable to see any colours.

For most people, colour blind is inherited. That means it is passed down from the biological parents — from the mother or birthing parent in the most common red-green forms of colour blind. But people can also acquire colour vision deficiency later in life due to medical conditions or other reasons.

There are three main categories of colour blind which are red-green, blue-yellow, and total colour blind.

What are the symptoms of colour blind?

Difficulty Distinguishing Colours
People with colour blind often have trouble differentiating between certain colours, especially red and green, which are commonly affected in red-green colour blind.

Colour Confusion
People may confuse colours, such as mistaking red for green, blue for yellow or vice versa.

Reduced Colour Perception
Colour blind people may have a reduced ability to perceive and distinguish between certain colours.

Mistakes in Colour Identification
They may make mistakes when identifying colours, which can affect everyday tasks like choosing clothing or scraves colours to match with cloth or interpreting colour-coded information.
